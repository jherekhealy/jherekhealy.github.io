About the cover: implied volatility fractals!

As you will have probably already noticed, the front and back pictures of my book are fractals. But they are not just any fractal. They are generated by coloring the number of iterations needed to solve the implied volatility of a vanilla option of a given price in the complex plane, using a root finding algorithm like Newton’s method or Householder’s method. Each point corresponds to a complex implied volatility guess and the more iterations needed, the darker is the point.

Fifth Edition E-Book

The fifth edition of the book is now available in e-book PDF format on Lulu. It is soon going to be availabel in color hardcover or paperback on Amazon.

This color edition brings the following minor updates:

  • Vanilla option pricing under the spot piecewise-lognormal model has been reworked and now include newer approximations.
  • Asian option pricing also includes newer approximations.
  • Time-dependent piecewise-constant stochastic volatility vanilla pricing, calibration and simulation.
  • A small section on forward variance model calibration.

Example code for a set of important algorithms detailed in this book are available on the github Julia code repository AQFED.jl. The code is experimental, not production grade and subject to changes. More will appear progressively, along with a more precise documentation.

Fourth Edition in Colors

The fourth edition of the book is now available on Amazon (UK, DE, FR) as hardcover, printed in colors.

Some chapters have been split and their order has been updated. In terms of content, vanilla option pricing under the spot piecewise-lognormal model has been reworked and now includes newer approximations. The pricing of American options under negative rates adds the latest developments of Andersen and Lake. The sections on the finite difference method have been significantly updated, especially around grid stretching, payoff smoothing, the solution of the linear complementary problem under negative rates, and explicit super-time-stepping schemes. A variety of arbitrage-free representations for implied volatilities has been added, as well as an in-depth comparison of the various techniques on a set of concrete market data. Finally the chapter on stochastic volatility now includes simulation and calibration of rough volatility models. More minor changes include the addition of the SABR approximation for basket option prices along with new details on the Black-Scholes approximations, a more careful summation for Monte-Carlo, the Jacobian based Vega for stochastic volatility models, fixing typos around the pricing of variance swaps, which also now presents more challenging examples.
